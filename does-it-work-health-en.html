"Does This Actually Work?" — How to Handle the Trust Objection in Health Product Sales

The hardest question in wellness sales, and why the agents who answer it best never make a health claim.

Why This Objection Cuts Deeper Than a Price Objection

When a client asks "does this actually work?", they're not just asking about the product. They're asking whether they can trust you.

Health and wellness products carry a unique burden in the sales conversation: almost every client has already bought something that didn't work. A supplement that made no difference. A program they quit after two weeks. A device that collected dust. By the time they're talking to you, their default assumption is skepticism — not because of you personally, but because of every sales conversation they've had before you.

This is what makes "does this actually work?" so different from a price objection. A price objection is a negotiation. A trust objection is a test — and the way you respond to it either passes or fails it instantly.

The short version, if you're skimming: - "Does this work?" is a trust question, not a product question - Answering it with health claims ("clinically proven to...") almost always makes skepticism worse - The most effective response acknowledges the skepticism as legitimate, then redirects to specifics - Price objections in health sales are almost always trust objections in disguise

What Not to Say (And Why It Backfires)

Before the scripts that work, it's worth being clear about the response that almost every new rep reaches for — and why it consistently makes things worse.

"It's clinically proven / doctor-recommended / backed by research."

This sounds like the right answer. It isn't. Here's why:

Every health product that has ever been sold uses some version of this language. Clients know this. The moment you reach for "clinically proven," you sound like every other rep they've ever said no to. It doesn't build trust — it confirms the pattern they were already expecting.

There's also a compliance dimension: specific health benefit claims ("this product lowers blood pressure," "this will help you lose 20 pounds") are regulated territory in the US, and making them without proper qualification creates legal exposure. The most effective sales approach and the safest compliance approach happen to be the same thing: don't make the claim. Describe the experience instead.

The Three Responses That Actually Work

Response 1: Validate the Skepticism First

This is counterintuitive, but it's the single most effective move in health product sales.

Script: "Honestly? That's exactly the right question to ask, and I'd be worried if you didn't. There's a lot of noise in this space, and most people have tried something that didn't do what it said. What I can tell you is what I've seen — not what I promise it will do for you, because that's something you'd have to experience yourself."

What this does: it separates you from every rep who leads with claims. The client expected you to defend the product. Instead, you agreed with their skepticism. That shift changes the dynamic of the entire conversation.

Response 2: Redirect to Specifics, Not Generalities

After validating, move the conversation from "does it work in general" to "what are you specifically hoping to address."

Script: "Can I ask — what would 'working' look like for you? I want to understand what you're actually trying to solve, because that changes what I'd even recommend."

What this does: most clients have a very specific concern (energy levels, sleep quality, a particular health goal) that they haven't named yet. When you ask this question, two things happen: you get information you need to give a genuinely useful recommendation, and the client feels heard rather than sold to. That's a Dale Carnegie move — get them talking about what they want before you say anything about what you have.

Response 3: Use Social Proof Without Making Claims

After you understand what they're trying to solve, you can share experiences — carefully framed.

Script: "A lot of people I work with come to me with something similar. What I typically hear back after a few weeks is [describe experience, not outcome — e.g., 'they say they feel more consistent energy through the afternoon' rather than 'it gives you more energy']. I can't promise that's what you'll experience, but that's what comes up most often."

The framing distinction here matters: "they say they feel" is a reported experience. "It gives you" is a claim. One is honest and defensible. The other creates expectations you can't control and, in some jurisdictions, legal exposure you don't want.

The Price Objection Is Usually a Trust Objection

When a client says "it's too expensive," especially for a health product, there's almost always a trust layer underneath: I'm not sure it works, so I'm not sure it's worth this price.

The mistake most reps make is responding to the price. The client who says "this is too expensive" and secretly means "I'm not convinced it'll do anything" will not be moved by a payment plan or a discount — because the real objection hasn't been addressed.

Script for a price objection with a trust layer: "I hear you on the price — it's a real consideration. Can I ask something first? If you were completely confident it would do what you're hoping for, would the price still feel like the barrier? I want to make sure I'm actually answering the right question."

If they say yes, the price is the real objection — address it directly. If they pause and say "well, no, if I knew it worked..." — you've surfaced the real issue, and now you can use Response 1 or 2 above.

What Makes Health Product Sales Different From Insurance or Real Estate

In insurance, the product is standardized — a whole life policy from one carrier is legally defined and regulated. In real estate, the product is a physical property clients can see and compare. In health and wellness sales, the product is experiential — the client can't verify the outcome before they buy, and neither can you promise one.

This is why the trust objection hits harder here than in almost any other sales category, and why the standard "overcome the objection with proof" playbook backfires. You're not overcoming skepticism with evidence. You're building a relationship where the client feels safe enough to try something they can't verify in advance.

That requires a completely different skill set — and it's exactly the kind of nuanced conversation that benefits most from structured practice before you're in front of a real client.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I share testimonials from other clients? Be careful. In the US, the FTC has specific guidelines on testimonials for health products — particularly that you must disclose if results are not typical, and you cannot imply that a testimonial represents what a new customer should expect. Sharing experiences is fine; implying guaranteed outcomes is not. When in doubt, frame it as "what I hear from people I work with" rather than "here's what our customers report."

What if the client has tried a similar product before and it didn't work? This is actually an opportunity, not an obstacle. Ask them specifically what they tried, and what they were hoping it would do. This gives you information, shows genuine curiosity, and often reveals that the previous product was addressing something different from what they actually needed.

How do I handle someone who is openly cynical — "all these products are scams"? Don't argue. Agree with the general sentiment: "You're right that there's a lot of bad product in this space." Then ask: "What would it take for you to feel like something was genuinely worth trying?" You're not defending the industry — you're finding out what this specific person's threshold for trust actually is.

Practice the Conversation You'll Actually Have

The "does this work?" objection almost never comes alone. It comes mid-conversation, after the client has already heard your introduction, right when you think the conversation is going well. Handling it in the moment — without getting defensive, without reaching for a claim — is a skill that needs to be built before it matters.
