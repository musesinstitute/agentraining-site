"I Need to Think About It" — What Insurance Agents Should Say Next

The most common close-killer in insurance sales, and the three responses that actually work.

Why This Objection Is Different From All the Others

Most sales objections tell you something specific: the premium is too high, the product is too complicated, the timing isn't right. Those objections give you something to work with.

"I need to think about it" tells you almost nothing — which is exactly why it's so dangerous.

It could mean: - The client genuinely needs time to process a complex decision - They're not convinced but don't want to say so directly - A spouse or family member needs to be involved - They have an unspoken concern they haven't voiced yet - They're simply being polite and have already decided no

An agent who treats all five of these the same way will lose most of them. The right response depends entirely on which one it actually is — and that means your first job, before saying anything else, is to find out.

The short version, if you're skimming: - "I need to think about it" is rarely about thinking — it's usually about something unsaid - The worst response is to push for urgency ("This offer expires soon!") - The best response is a calm, curious question that surfaces what's really going on - Experienced agents lose this close most often, not new ones — because they stop listening before the client finishes

The Three Responses That Actually Work

Response 1: The Calm Clarifying Question

This works when you're not sure which version of "think about it" you're dealing with.

Script: "Of course — this is an important decision and I want you to feel completely comfortable. Can I ask: is there a specific part of what we discussed that you'd like to think through more? I want to make sure I've answered everything before we leave it."

What this does: it signals you're not going to pressure them, and it opens a door for the real objection to come out. Most clients, when they feel no pressure, will tell you what's actually holding them back.

What to listen for after you ask: if they name something specific ("I'm not sure about the premium amount"), you now have something to work with. If they stay vague ("I just need some time"), move to Response 2.

Response 2: The Assumption-Free Explore

This works when the client is clearly hesitant but hasn't told you why.

Script: "That makes complete sense. Before I let you go — and I'm not trying to rush you at all — I just want to make sure I haven't left anything unclear. Is there anything about the coverage, the cost, or how the policy works that didn't quite land the way you expected?"

What this does: it gives the client three specific doors to walk through (coverage, cost, how it works), without making them feel like they have to justify themselves. People who say "I need to think about it" because of price will often move toward cost when given permission.

Response 3: The Future-Pacing Question

This works when the client genuinely seems thoughtful rather than avoidant — and you want to keep the conversation alive without pressuring them.

Script: "Absolutely. When you're thinking it over, what would it look like for this to feel like the right decision? I want to make sure whatever you decide, you have everything you need to feel confident about it."

What this does: it shifts the conversation from "should I do this" to "what would make this the right choice" — a subtle but important reframe. It also positions you as someone helping them think, not someone trying to close them.

What Not to Do

Don't create artificial urgency. "This rate is only locked in until Friday" or "I can't guarantee this premium next time we meet" — unless these are genuinely true and material to the decision, they damage trust the moment the client senses pressure. And they always sense it.

Don't summarize everything again. Restating all the benefits after a client says "I need to think about it" tells them you weren't listening to their signal. They've already heard the pitch.

Don't ask "what would it take to earn your business today?" This is the single most transparent closing line in sales, and clients who've heard it before — which is most of them — will close down completely.

Why Experienced Agents Lose This Close More Than New Ones

New agents who hear "I need to think about it" get nervous and sometimes push too hard. That's a fixable problem with practice.

Experienced agents have a subtler version of the same mistake: they've heard this objection so many times that they stop actually listening to this particular client saying it. They run a mental script instead of staying curious.

The agent who hears "I need to think about it" and immediately thinks "okay, this is the think-about-it objection, I'll use response B" has already made an assumption. The agent who hears it and thinks "interesting — I wonder which version of this it is" is going to close a lot more deals.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I follow up if the client says they need time? Yes — but not the next day. Give it the amount of time they implied (if they said "I'll think about it this week," wait a week). When you follow up, don't open with the product. Ask how they're feeling about it, and listen before you say anything else.

What if a spouse needs to be involved? This is a separate objection hiding inside "I need to think about it." The right response is to name it: "Is your spouse someone who'd want to be part of this conversation? I'm happy to set up a time when we can all talk together." Trying to close without the decision-maker present almost never works.

What if I've already tried a clarifying question and they still won't open up? Some clients have decided no and are being polite. The most professional thing you can do is leave cleanly: "I appreciate the time — if you have any questions when you're thinking it over, I'm always available." A client who feels no pressure after the meeting is far more likely to call you back than one who felt pushed.
